So I just did a group reading of Nietzsche's The Anti-Christ[1] over on the Deleuze and Guattari Quarantine Collective Discord server and there was a specific aphorism that stood out to all of us. It was aphorism 57 and it contained some of the most interesting and controversial statements from Nietzsche from the perspective of liberatory politics and, in my opinion, sums up the entire book.

This aphorism might come off as conservative drivel if you read it in the wrong light and do not take any care to look past the phrasing he uses to see the kindness behind everything he says. Keep this in mind when reading all of Nietzsche or you will not be able to understand him. He is not meant to be swallowed easy or to be able to fit neatly between your existing thoughts. Just try to hold back that initial repulsion and sit with him for a while, believing that he is the joyous, strong, and empathetic man he says he is.

Here is aphorism 57 in full, but note that it is quite long (2.5 pages on the PDF) and (almost) all of it will be quoted individually below, so it is completely okay to skip it, but it may serve you well to familiarize you with the whole thing with complete context. I actually typed it all out by hand in order to force myself to really read through it slowly and carefully.

The Antichrist: Aphorism 57
If you measure the Christian goal against the goal of the law book of Manu - if you examine these starkly antithetical goals under a strong light - you will catch the unholiness of the means Christians use in flagrante. The critic of Christianity cannot help but make Christianity look despicable. - A law book like that of Manu comes about in the same way as every good book of law: it summarizes the experience, shrewdness, and experiments in morality of many centuries, it draws a conclusion, nothing more. This sort of codification presupposes an insight into the following fact: the way that a slowly and expensively acquired truth becomes authoritative is fundamentally different from the way that this truth would be proven. A book of law never describes the uses, the reasons, and the casuistry in the prehistory of a law: this would make it lose its imperative tone, the 'thou shalt', the presupposition for being obeyed. This is precisely where the problem is. - At a certain point in the development of a people, its most circumspect (which is to say far-sighted and hind-sighted) class declares that the experience according to which life should be conducted - that is, can be conducted -, is over. Its goal is to harvest the richest and most perfect crop possible from the ages of experiment and bad experience. Accordingly what now needs to be prevented at all cost is any further experimentation, the continuation of values in a fluid state, scrutiny, selection, and criticism of values in infinitum. A double wall is set up against this: first: revelation, the claim that the reasoning behind the law does not have a mortal provenance, has not been slowly and painstakingly looked for and discovered, but instead had a divine origin, arriving whole, complete, without history, a gift, a miracle, simply communicated...[sic] And second: tradition, which is to say the claim that the law existed from time immemorial, that it is irreverent to cast doubt on it, a crime against the ancestors. The authority of the law is grounded in the theses: God gave it, the ancestors lived by it. - The higher reasoning behind the procedure like this involves the intention to push back conciousness gradually - to identify some life as the proper one (which is to say one that has been proved through immense and highly filtered experiences) and to prevent people from becoming concious of it: to achieve a perfect automatism of the instinct, - this is the presupposition of every type of mastery, of every type of perfection in the art of life. To prepare a book of law in the style of Manu means to give a people the right to become master one day, to become perfect, - to aspire the highest art of life. To this end, it must be made unconscious: this is the goal of every holy lie. - Caste-order, the most supreme, domineering law, is just the sanction of a natural order, natural lawfulness Par Excellence - chance and 'modern ideas' have no sway over it. In every healthy society, three mutually conditioning physiological types separate out and gravitate in different directions, each one having its own hygiene, its own area of work, its own feelings of perfection and field of mastery, Nature, not Manu, separates out predominantly spiritual people from people characterized by muscular and temperamental strength from a third group of people who are not distinguished in either way, the mediocre, - the latter being the great number the first being the exceptions. The highest caste - which I call the few -, being the perfect caste, also has the privilege of the few: this includes representing happiness, beauty, goodness on earth. Only the most spiritual human beings are allowed to be beautiful: only among them is goodness not a weakness. Pulchrum est paucorum hominum[Beauty is for the few]: goodness is a privilege. On the other hand, nothing can be tolerated less in this type than ugly manners or a pessimistic look, an eye that makes things ugly -, or even an indignation over the way of the world. Indignation is the privilege of the Chandala; pessimism too. 'The world Is perfect' - this is how the instinct of the most spiritual people speaks, the yes-saying instinct: 'imperfection, every type of being that is beneath us, distance, the pathos of distance, even the Chandala belongs to this perfection'. The most spiritual people, being the strongest, find their happiness where other people would find their downfall: in labyrinths, in harshness towards themselves and towards others, in trials; they take pleasure in self-overcoming: asceticism is their nature, requirement, instinct. They see difficult tasks as a privilege, they relax by playing with burdens that would crush other people...[sic] Knowledge - a form of asceticism. - They are the most admirable type of people: which does not prevent them from being the most cheerful, the kindest, they do not rule because they want to, but rather because they exist, they are not free to be second. - The ones who are second: these are the custodians of the law, the guardians of order and security, these are the noble warriors, this is above all the king, as the highest formula of the warrior, judge, and preserver of the law. The ones who are second are the executives of the most spiritual people; they are closest to them, belong to them, and take over everything crude in the works of ruling - they are their attendants, their right hand, their best pupils. - In all of this, to say it again, there is nothing arbitrary, nothing 'contrived'; anything different is contrived, - contrived to put nature to shame...[sic] Caste-order, order of rank, is just a formula for the supreme law of life itself, splitting off into three types is necessary for the preservation of society, to make the higher and highest types possible, - unequal rights are the condition for any rights at all. - A right is a privilege. Everyone finds this privilege in his own type of being. Let us not underestimate the privileges of the mediocre. Life becomes increasingly difficult the higher up you go, - it gets colder, there are more responsibilities. A high culture is a pyramid: it needs a broad base, its first presupposition is a strongly and healthily consolidated mediocrity. Craft, trade, farming, science, most of art - in a word, employment can only really function on the basis of a mediocrity of ability and desire: this sort of thing would be out of place among exceptional people, the associated instinct would contradict both aristocratism and anarchism. To be a public utility, a wheel, a function - you need to be destined for this by nature: it is not society but rather the type of happiness that the vast majority of people cannot rise above that make them intelligent machines. For the mediocre, mediocrity is a happiness; mastery of the one thing, specialization as a natural instinct. It would be completely unworthy of a more profound spirit to have any objection to mediocrity as such. Mediocrity is needed before there can be exceptions: it is the condition for a high culture. When an exceptional person treats a mediocre one more delicately than he treats himself and his equals, this is not just courtesy of the heart[A line from Goethe's novel Elective Affinities], - it is his duty...[sic] Who do I hate most among the rabble today? The socialist rabble, the Chandala-apostles who undermine workers' instinct and pleasures, their feelings of modesty about their little existences, - who make them jealous, who teach them revenge...[sic] Injustice is never a matter of unequal rights, it is a matter of claiming 'equal' rights...[sic] What is bad? But I have already said it: everything that comes from weakness, from jealousy, from revenge. - The anarchist and the Christian are descended from the same lineage...[sic]

Let's start out with the first two sentences

If you measure the Christian goal against the goal of the law book of Manu - if you examine these starkly antithetical goals under a strong light - you will catch the unholiness of the means Christians use in flagrante. The critic of Christianity cannot help but make Christianity look despicable.

Nietzsche is immediately setting up that he will be comparing the goals of Christianity compared to those of the book of Manu. Now, Nietzsche's use of the book of Manu is complicated and rather historically inaccurate (I am not an expert but the Wikipedia page seems to contradict a lot of what Nietzsche said about the book in the preceding paragraphs, it is also very likely that Nietzsche didn't have access to an accurate translation when he wrote this in the 1890's), so it is best to attempt to look at what Nietzsche is trying to convey rather than looking at the particulars. He might as well be comparing the Bible to the Great Shiftafoonafally Scribes[2]. What he is doing is positioning the book of Manu as an example of using the 'thou shalt' religious language to perpetuate the Will to Power, or, to produce a society that lasts and continues functioning healthily, which he will outline later. He will go on to say that the Christian laws bring about self-destruction and that the law of Manu laws bring about self-preservation and self-overcoming. Through the book of Manu, Nietzsche argues, the sinister nature behind Christian laws becomes readily apparent because the book of Manu is using the same means as the Christians use but with a completely different goal.

Now, what immediately stood out to me was the value he placed on self-preservation here. Does Nietzsche not care most about self-overcoming and progression? My interpretation is that when he refers to self-preservation, he is referring to that very progression toward mastery.

To back this up, we will go out of order and jump to aphorism 58 before quickly going back to analyzing aphorism 57:

The Antichrist: Aphorism 58

...The Christian and the anarchist: both are decadents, neither one can do anything except dissolve... both have instincts of moral hatred against everything that stands, that stands tall, that has endurance, that promises life a future

Here, Nietzsche brings "future" into "endurance", showing that he is not referring to a static zombified preservation, which was the original interpretation I had. So to re-iterate, he is not saying something radically different than his other works here, even though it may sound like it. He is positing that the goal of the Christian laws is to make the Will to Power go against itself and that the goal of the book of Manu is to proliferate and maintain the Will to Power, which is not the same as keeping it stagnant.

Just as a quick note: the comparison of Anarchists to Christians here might come off as alarming to some of the leftists here, but try to sit with it and not be angry immediately, we will get to it later, but I will not be negating your initial repulsion and saying he was kidding the whole time. This is very much something to take seriously and is hopefully going to become easier to swallow when you understand his motivations and his project.

Now here is where a lot gets challenging:

A law book like that of Manu comes about in the same way as every good book of law: it summarizes the experience, shrewdness, and experiments in morality of many centuries, it draws a conclusion, nothing more.

This is important to keep in mind. The laws of morality in the book of Manu, and every other book of its type, is the end-result of years and years of history. Finding the morality that allows a society to move forward is a historical act, but as Nietzsche says, that history is necessarily obfuscated:

At a certain point in the development of a people, its most circumspect (which is to say far-sighted and hind-sighted) class declares that the experience according to which life should be conducted - that is, can be conducted -, is over. Its goal is to harvest the richest and most perfect crop possible from the ages of experiment and bad experience. Accordingly what now needs to be prevented at all cost is any further experimentation, the continuation of values in a fluid state, scrutiny, selection, and criticism of values in infinitum. A double wall is set up against this: first: revelation, the claim that the reasoning behind the law does not have a mortal provenance, has not been slowly and painstakingly looked for and discovered, but instead had a divine origin, arriving whole, complete, without history, a gift, a miracle, simply communicated...[sic] And second: tradition, which is to say the claim that the law existed from time immemorial, that it is irreverent to cast doubt on it, a crime against the ancestors. The authority of the law is grounded in the theses: God gave it, the ancestors lived by it.

This mechanism is the very Revaluation of all Values that Nietzsche was hoping to induce! After "ages of experiment and bad experience", a law is "discovered" that is designed to undermine the current values in order to set up stronger, more stable, and life affirming ones in their place. When this happens, history is rewritten as if none of the experimenting ever happened and a paradoxical relationship is born between "tradition" and "divine origin". Here, it is easy to see that Nietzsche is showing a more positive, thoughtful, and affirming approach to the Revaluation of all Values that Nietzsche outlines in The Genealogy of Morals where slave morality pulled the rug out from under master morality and obscured its history.

On the Genealogy of Morality: Treatise 1: Aphorism 7

...the Jews, that priestly people who in the end were only able to achieve satisfaction from their enemies and conquerors through a radical revaluation of their values, hence through an act of the most spiritual revenge. This way alone was suitable for a priestly people, the people of the most deeply repressed priestly vengefulness. It was the Jews who countered the aristocratic value equation (good = noble = powerful = beautiful = happy = beloved of God) by daring with fear-inspiring consistency to invert it, and held on to it by the teeth of the most abysmal hatred (the hatred of the impotent), namely "only the miserable are the good, the poor, impotent, lowly alone are the good, the suffering, deprived, sick, ugly are also the only pious ones, the only ones blessed by God, there is blessedness for them only- whereas you, you noble and mighty, you are in all eternity the evil, cruel, lustful, insatiable, godless, you will also eternally be the unblessed, accursed and damned!"

Here we see a lot of obvious parallels, but what I want to focus on is the difference. Here, we see that "the Jews" (a group Nietzsche associates with decadence and destruction in the same way he does Christians) were able to enact a Revaluation of all Values by simply negating master morality. The result of this is not a new master morality that overcomes the limitations and contradictions of the original but instead just a denial of everything that is of higher spirit than they are. While the book of Manu, supposedly (again, the actual book is not of importance here), seeks to find a new outlet for the Will to Power and provides a deep affirmation of life. Another way to think about this is that both attempt to lock in a new set of morals that are both complete and, at the same time, having a long history of tradition, but while Manu aims to create morals that are self-preserving and lead to continued overcoming within the system, Christian morals do very little to provide new paths to for the Will to Power to express itself (several paths still open up by accident as Nietzsche points out in his other works, but nevertheless, the goal is still infantile, angry, nihilistic negation of everything that affirms life). That is the main difference. One is an act of pure hatred and destruction and the other is an act of love and deep affirmation of life that only prevents the will to power from turning against itself. One is the result of ressentiment[3] and the other is the result of living and experimenting.

The higher reasoning behind the procedure like this involves the intention to push back conciousness gradually - to identify some life as the proper one (which is to say one that has been proved through immense and highly filtered experiences) and to prevent people from becoming concious of it: to achieve a perfect automatism of the instinct, - this is the presupposition of every type of mastery, of every type of perfection in the art of life. To prepare a book of law in the style of Manu means to give a people the right to become master one day, to become perfect, - to aspire the highest art of life. To this end, it must be made unconscious: this is the goal of every holy lie.

This contrasts with the way Nietzsche describes Kant's categorical imperative earlier in this book:

The Antichrist: Aphorism 11

...To think that people did not sense the mortal danger posed by Kant's categorical imperative!...[sic] The theologian instinct was the only thing that came to its defense! - When the instinct of life compels us to act, pleasure proves that the act is right: and this nihilist with the intestines of a Christian dogmatist saw pleasure as an objection...[sic] What could be more destructive than working, thinking, feeling, without any inner need, any deeply personal choice, any pleasure? As an automaton of 'duty'? It is almost the recipe for decadence, even for idiocy...[sic] Kant became an idiot.

Here we see a description of the theologian method of creating values: anything that's natural, anything that's pleasurable, anything that's animal, is all prohibited. In other words, the Christian laws intend to create an instinct that rejects all instincts. Nietzsche is not saying that Christians have gone "beyond instinct" but rather that they create instincts that hide and shame the actual instincts "hidden" behind all of their "concious", "rational", "transcendent" actions:

The Antichrist: Aphorism 39 [Bolding added by me for emphasis]

...to put it more rigorously, the whole idea of spiritual causation is false. To reduce Christianity, to reduce being Christian to a set of claims taken to be true, to a simple phenomenalism of conciousness, is to negate Christianity.... Examined more closely and in spite of all 'belief', they have been governed only by instincts,- and what instincts they are! - In every age... 'belief' has just been a cloak, a cover, a curtain behind which the instincts play their game... In the world of christian representations, nothing that happens has any bearing on the reality: on the contrary, we have recognized the instinct of hatred against every reality as the driving, the only driving element at the root of Christianity.

It is very important to recognize that Nietzsche doesn't believe in anything beyond the instincts. So when he critiques a system for shaming instincts, he is talking about the instincts turning against themselves (he also calls this the Will to Death, decadence, Will to Nothing, etc). The book of Manu encourages higher instincts discovered from ages of experimentation, while the Christian laws are designed to obfuscate the instincts that created them and still rule over them and, at the same time, wage war against everything with the appearance of instincts. This way, a permanent structure is created using that same two-walled strategy Nietzsche outlined that actively curbs any potential overcoming. It is a structure that poisons everything within it and cannot go away.

Caste-order, the most supreme, domineering law, is just the sanction of a natural order, natural lawfulness Par Excellence - chance and 'modern ideas' have no sway over it. In every healthy society, three mutually conditioning physiological types separate out and gravitate in different directions, each one having its own hygiene, its own area of work, its own feelings of perfection and field of mastery, Nature, not Manu, separates out predominantly spiritual people from people characterized by muscular and temperamental strength from a third group of people who are not distinguished in either way, the mediocre, - the latter being the great number the first being the exceptions. The highest caste - which I call the few

A number of you might immediately be repulsed by the use of "natural order" and "natural lawfulness" here, especially when referring to a caste system. You might be thinking, "Man this Nietzsche guy is just like what me mother always said he was! I should have never gotten into philosophy!". And, again, I will ask you to try to sit with this for a moment instead of freaking out, it's okay I promise. He understands how combative and shocking he is being here, he knows what he's doing to his reader.

Remember he said that the book of Manu is the result of experimentation and active-living. It is a list of codes that reproduce a subject who fits them without fail. Even the mediocre (what Nietzsche usually refers to as The Herd) have an important role that allows self-mastery. If you are thinking of the Indian caste-system or the caste-system that was produced in America as a necessity of chattel slavery, this is not that. Here everything is autonomous, there is no ressentiment (or if there is, it's quickly dissipating), it is all instinctual and every life is beautiful and affirmed. This is not to say that this is meant to be a utopian vision of a caste system, it is just to say that we do not currently have this system because it is deeply incompatible with Christian morality, as he will demonstrate by showing the modern manifestations of Christian morality that appear within supposedly liberatory projects.

For those familiar with Nietzsche, this framing, outside of the unique word choice, will be familiar.
There are the few: the masters, the noble, etc. The spiritually strong and the physically strong. They produce values by the nature of their being.
And the many: the herd, the mediocre, etc. The ones who cannot produce beyond themselves. You can think of The Herd as the ones who have no drive to go beyond the limitations of what is exchangeable within the society. They just want comfort and safety, everything else is inconceivable.

It is important to note that master morality and herd morality (NOT slave morality) are not trying to totalize individuals but rather to express the dominant drives within them. Both are produced. Neither one is "better" than the other. As Nietzsche says, they all have their own "hygiene", "area of work", and "feelings of perfection and field of mastery". When he says this process is natural, he is only pointing out that only few people can be special and that special people are disruptive to their environment in a way the masses (the mediocre) are not. He is not saying one is better than the other or naturally deserves a higher position. He is saying that the book of Manu is trying to codify the results of that process. It didn't just appear out of thin air.

being the perfect caste, also has the privilege of the few: this includes representing happiness, beauty, goodness on earth. Only the most spiritual human beings are allowed to be beautiful: only among them is goodness not a weakness. Pulchrum est paucorum hominum[Beauty is for the few]: goodness is a privilege. On the other hand, nothing can be tolerated less in this type than ugly manners or a pessimistic look, an eye that makes things ugly -, or even an indignation over the way of the world. Indignation is the privilege of the Chandala; pessimism too. 'The world Is perfect' - this is how the instinct of the most spiritual people speaks, the yes-saying instinct: 'imperfection, every type of being that is beneath us, distance, the pathos of distance, even the Chandala belongs to this perfection'... In all of this, to say it again, there is nothing arbitrary, nothing 'contrived'; anything different is contrived, - contrived to put nature to shame...[sic] Caste-order, order of rank, is just a formula for the supreme law of life itself, splitting off into three types is necessary for the preservation of society, to make the higher and highest types possible, - unequal rights are the condition for any rights at all. - A right is a privilege. Everyone finds this privilege in his own type of being.

This part mentions his idea of "the pathos of distance". This concept comes up in some of his work but I think the easiest place to look at is Genealogy of Morals. I don't think its controversial at all to say that these books very much go hand in hand.

The pathos of nobility and distance, as I said, the lasting and dominating overall and basic feeling of a higher ruling order in relation to a lower order, to a "below" - that is the origin of the opposition "good " and "bad." (The master's right to give names goes so far as to allow us to conceive of the origin of language itself as an expression of power on the part of the rulers; they say "this is thus and such," they seal every thing and occurrence with a sound and thereby take possession of it, so to speak.

The pathos of distance is the ability of the few (the noble, the strong, the high-spirited) to create laws out from themselves without relying on a base to negate or pervert. This might seem obvious but it is very important. The pathos of distance is what allows life-affirming value creation to happen at all. The herd cannot create new instincts without just negating what the nobles say. This is why the christian morality, which is a value system where the herd wins over the few, is simply a negation: they say that the noble are evil so by opposition everything that is the opposite of the nobility (the weak, the mediocre) must be the good. It is very important to, once again, keep in mind that Nietzsche is aware that this reversal is also natural and occurs over time due to the contradictions between castes. He is simply choosing to give precedence to the one he admires and says is the original. This is rather obvious to those who have read this book and know what it is meant to accomplish. As the book is attempting to create its own Revaluation of all values, he is attempting to redefine "truth", "virtue", and "nature" to suit his goal. That is his "holy lie".

As will come up at the end of this aphorism, here is where Nietzsche betrays his methodology right at the beginning of the book, employing the 'thou shalt' imperative of the book of Manu

The Antichrist: Aphorism 2

What is good? - Everything that enhances people's feelings of power, will to power, power itself.

What is bad? - Everything stemming from weakness.

What is happiness? - the feeling that power is growing, that some resistance has been overcome.

Not contentedness, but more power; not peace, but war; not virtue, but prowess (virtue in the style of the Renaissance, virtù, moraline-free virtue)

The weak and the failures should perish: first principle of our love of humanity. And they should be helped to do this.

What is more harmful than any vice? - Active pity for all failures and weakness - Christianity...[sic]

The entire project is an attempt to shift values and morality in order to begin, once again, willing special, noble, subversive, powerful, people into existence[4]. This is why Nietzsche is using words in the way he does, it is very much intentional. And it is also very much intentional that is will be off-putting to most readers, his work was never trying to be palatable.

Let us not underestimate the privileges of the mediocre. Life becomes increasingly difficult the higher up you go, - it gets colder, there are more responsibilities. A high culture is a pyramid: it needs a broad base, its first presupposition is a strongly and healthily consolidated mediocrity. Craft, trade, farming, science, most of art - in a word, employment can only really function on the basis of a mediocrity of ability and desire: this sort of thing would be out of place among exceptional people, the associated instinct would contradict both aristocratism and anarchism. To be a public utility, a wheel, a function - you need to be destined for this by nature: it is not society but rather the type of happiness that the vast majority of people cannot rise above that make them intelligent machines. For the mediocre, mediocrity is a happiness; mastery of the one thing, specialization as a natural instinct. It would be completely unworthy of a more profound spirit to have any objection to mediocrity as such. Mediocrity is needed before there can be exceptions: it is the condition for a high culture. When an exceptional person treats a mediocre one more delicately than he treats himself and his equals, this is not just courtesy of the heart[A line from Goethe's novel Elective Affinities], - it is his duty

For Nietzsche, the mediocrity is produced through their very happiness of not being extraordinary. They are not jealous, they are not seeking revenge, they are autonomous beings with every decision being unconscious. The mediocre are still masters here. They are still empowered. The only difference is that they do not create values, they also have no drive to. It is only when the man of ressentiment, a priest, comes in to tell them they deserve better and should not be happy, that the herd begins lashing out and brings up the values of decadence.

For me, a pretty strange thing here stands out: the allusion to "duty". Does Nietzsche not despise the concept? Does he not seek to bring it under instinct?

My answer is that I am fairly confident with is that he is pointing out the preserving aspect of this action. This society requires constant upkeep, like every other society, and it does not hide it. The extraordinary, the few, must treat the herd with care in order for the society to maintain. This, as everything great, should become unconscious, but nevertheless it is a duty because it takes up such an "imperative tone"; the entire society relies on it.

And finally we come to the final and most hostile part:

Who do I hate most among the rabble today? The socialist rabble, the Chandala-apostles who undermine workers' instinct and pleasures, their feelings of modesty about their little existences, - who make them jealous, who teach them revenge...[sic] Injustice is never a matter of unequal rights, it is a matter of claiming 'equal' rights...[sic] What is bad? But I have already said it: everything that comes from weakness, from jealousy, from revenge. - The anarchist and the Christian are descended from the same lineage.

The class antagonism comes from the... Liberatory politics aimed to resolve class antagonism? Huh?

Yes. The theologian instinct found in anarchist and socialist is this: they are looking for an endpoint where overcoming is no longer possible. They do not care for preservation or finding sources of empowerment and strength. They are the same, angry, lashing out, sick people who the priests took advantage of. Now that I said that, let me backpedal a tiny bit. When Nietzsche talks about socialism and anarchism, keep in mind that he is talking about utopian schools of both as he never read Marx (or at least never commented on him). This is not to say that modern socialism and anarchism is awesome and Nietzsche is just talking about the old stuff. It is still very easy for anarchists especially to fall into this trap. Many anarchists are simply angry at the world and want to negate it. This is not to say I don't sympathize with the instinct, I very much do, but it dooms us to a society where self-overcoming and expression of the will to power is impossible. It literally leaves everyone impotent. Nietzsche does a very good job explaining this in Twilight of the Idols: both the Christian and the socialist find their salvation in a hinterland that's just out of reach (i.e heaven for Christians or the revolution for socialists). That allows them to condemn the world in pursuit of their goals and lose sight of everything that produced them, making them, once again, impotent, nihilistic, and self-hating. They also always choose an enemy to take revenge against that justifies whatever lashing out and revenge they need to do to feel a momentary feeling of power. In this way, they follow the same recipe for life-denial: hatred of life and investment into a world that does not exist, with a healthy dose of poisonous revenge they crave so much[5].

And that's the end of the aphorism! I hope it's understandable why this one through us for a loop during the reading, even though it seems simple when you break it up. It still changed how I thought about hierarchy and egalitarianism and also how I thought about Nietzsche's wider project. It really made me sit with the book and try to resolve how I would incorporate it without making it lose its punch but also without getting rid of all of the things I love about anarchism and leftist politics. I am glad to say that not all socialist projects fall into this trap. And, as would likely be surprising to Nietzsche, I would even say that modern anarchist ideas are the leaders in how to participate within society in a life-affirming, empowering, and sustainable way. Organizations like Crimethinc have been creating high quality articles that, among other things, give people tools to analyze, build, and be strong. One of my favorite articles is Against the Logic of the Guillotine. This is an article against the decadent instinct for revenge among anarchists and does a great job being empathetic (more than Nietzsche here, that's for sure) for the people calling for revenge while attempting to redirect them away from their Christian virtues. When rereading it, I was also pleasantly surprised to see it mentioned Nietzsche by name. Here is an excerpt from the last section[6] to end off this blog post:

Against the Logic of the Guillotine

Anarchism is a proposal for everyone regarding how we might go about improving our lives—workers and unemployed people, people of all ethnicities and genders and nationalities or lack thereof. The anarchist proposal is not in the interests of one currently existing group against another: it is not a way to enrich the poor at the expense of the rich, or to empower one ethnicity, nationality, or religion at others’ expense. That entire way of thinking is part of what we are trying to escape. All of the “interests” that supposedly characterize different categories of people are products of the prevailing order and must be transformed along with it, not preserved or pandered to. From our perspective, even the topmost positions of wealth and power that are available in the existing order are worthless. Nothing that capitalism and the state have to offer are of any value to us.

    Nietzsche is pretty infamous for making use of examples that are convenient for him without taking great care to represent the thing itself accurately. For example, in The Antichrist, his description of Buddhism is really lacking, but at the same time he is just using it to contrast Christianity, just as he's using the Book of Manu. He doesn't care if the Book of Manu or Buddhism works the way he says it does, and ideally, neither should you. It is best to recognize what he's pointing out in Christianity and the nature of decadence itself using those things, which where his analysis really shines.

    For a great introduction to what ressentiment is, Brooks from the DGQC has a great video explaining it: Partial Objects: What exactly is Ressentiment

    On the Genealogy of Morality also does a great job talking about it. Although it doesn't ever define it outright, the meaning is quite easy to glean from the way it is used. Ressentiment is when values are produced out of impotence. The classic example from On The Genealogy or Morality is:

    On the Genealogy of Morality: Treatise 1: Aphorism 13

    It does not seem strange that lambs bear a grudge against the great birds of prey: only this is no reason to hold it against the great birds of prey that they snatch themselves little lambs. And when the lambs say to each other "these birds of prey are evil; and whoever is a bird of prey to the least possible extent, rather even its opposite, a lamb - does he not have to be good?" then there is nothing wrong with this construction of an ideal, even if the birds of prey were to look upon this a bit sarcastically and perhaps say to themselves: "we do not bear a grudge against them, these good lambs, in fact we love them: nothing is tastier than a tender lamb.

    A value system created out of ressentiment can only ever be a negation: they see their "enemies" first and then construct their self-image and values out of the exact opposite of those who hurt them.

    Twilight of the Idols: Aphorism 34

    Christian and anarchist. - Anarchists are mouthpieces of a declining stratum of society; when they work themselves into a state of righteous indignation demanding 'rights', 'justice', 'equal rights', they are just acting under the pressure of their own lack of culture, which has no way of grasping why they really suffer, - what they lack in life...[sic] A powerful casual impulse is at work in them: it has to be someone's fault that they are not doing very well...[sic] And even the righteous indignation does them good, all poor devils like to complain, - it gives them a little rush of power. Complaining and grumbling can even give life a charm that makes it bearable: there is a subtle dose of revenge in every complaint; people blame the fact that they are doing badly (and sometimes even their badness) on those who are different, as if that constituted a wrong, an unauthorized privilege. 'If I am just canaille[the rabble, derived from 'dog'] then you should be too': out of this logic come revolutions. - Complaining never does any good: it comes from weakness. Whether you attribute your bad situation to other people or yourself (socialists take the former strategy and Christians, for instance, take the latter), it does not really make any difference. What is common to both (we could also say what is unworthy) is that somebody is supposedly to blame for your suffering - basically, that sufferers are prescribing themselves the honey of revenge for their suffering. The objects of this need for revenge (as a need for pleasure) are occasional causes: sufferers find opportunities everywhere to quench their thirst for vengeance, - and to say it again: if they are Christians, they find these opportunities in themselves...[sic] The Christian and the anarchist - both are decadents. - But when Christians condemn, libel, and denigrate the 'world', they are motivated by the same instinct that moves the socialist worker to condemn, libel, and denigrate society: even the 'Last Judgement' is the sweet consolation of revenge - the revolution that the socialist worker is waiting for, only a bit further off...[sic] Even the 'beyond' - what is a beyond for, if not to denigrate the here and now?...[sic]
